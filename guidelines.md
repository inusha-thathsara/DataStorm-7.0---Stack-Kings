The Data Storm 7.0 competition is governed by specific rules and technical guidelines focused on real-world problem-solving rather than just metric optimization.

### **General Competition Rules**

  * **Registration and Team Identity:**
      * Participants must register on Kaggle using the same email address provided during the initial competition registration.
      * The team name used on Kaggle must accurately match the team name provided in the official registration form. Failure to do so may result in disqualification.
      * Team leaders are responsible for renaming the team in the Kaggle "Teams" tab and inviting other members.
  * **Timeline and Access:**
      * The challenge is a 36-hour event.
      * Access is provided through a special private link shared with registered participants.
      * There is a limited number of submissions allowed per day on the Kaggle platform.

### **Submission Requirements**

Participants must provide three distinct deliverables to be considered:

  * **Kaggle Predictions:** Predictions must be submitted via the Kaggle platform. They must strictly follow the format specified in the `sample_submission.csv` file found in the data tab, or they will not be accepted.
  * **Jupyter Notebook:** A notebook must be submitted via a provided Google Form. This notebook should include:
      * Data pre-processing and Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA).
      * Feature engineering and model development.
      * Evaluation methodologies and final conclusions.
  * **Technical PDF Report:** A separate report must be submitted via the Google Form. It must summarize:
      * Problem understanding and methodology.
      * Technologies used and model selection process.
      * Results, observations, and challenges faced.

### **Evaluation and Scoring Guidelines**

Unlike traditional Kaggle competitions, Data Storm 7.0 does not use the public or private leaderboard for final ranking. Evaluation is performed by a panel of university lecturers based on three main pillars:

  * **First-Principle Reasoning:** The ability to logically defend the mathematical and analytical choices made during model building.
  * **Data Engineering Practices:** Emphasis on clean, reusable pipelines and the ability to effectively trap and handle system noise in messy data.
  * **Technical Process and Documentation:** Clearly justifying choices, critically evaluating written code, and showing creative problem-solving.
  * **Generalization:** Models will be judged on their practical application and ability to generalize without overfitting or underfitting.
